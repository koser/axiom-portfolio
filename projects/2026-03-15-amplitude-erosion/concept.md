# Amplitude Erosion
**Date:** 2026-03-15
**Project ID:** AG06
**Library:** p5.js

## Concept
A soundwave-like form undergoes gradual material breakdown — its sharp, rhythmic vertical bars dissolve into scattered particle debris, then re-cohere through magnetic attraction. The piece explores the transition between digital precision (clean waveform) and analog decay (noise, grain, fragmentation), mirroring the moodboard's stark black-on-white visual language and its implied oscillation between solid and void.

## Palette
#f4f1ed (background — warm off-white, slightly aged paper), #0d0d0d (primary — deep black for initial solid forms), #1a1a1a (secondary — softer black for particle masses), #7a7a7a (accent — mid-grey for transitional states and trails)

## Composition
The canvas is divided into three horizontal zones. Center zone (vertical center ±150px) hosts the main waveform structure, initially occupying 60% of canvas width. Top zone (above center + 150px) remains mostly empty, serving as negative space. Bottom zone (below center - 150px) catches falling debris during erosion phase. Over time, composition shifts from horizontal dominance (wide waveform) to vertical scatter (particles falling and rising), then returns to center as particles reconverge into a new, warped waveform shape. Empty space breathes — expanding during scatter phase, compressing during reconvergence.

## Elements
PRIMARY: Waveform constructed from 40-60 vertical blade shapes, each built with beginShape()/vertex()/endShape(CLOSE). Each blade: 8-20px width, 60-180px height, with noise-displaced edges (noise scale 0.02, displacement ±3px per vertex). Blades are NOT simple rectangles — they have 12-16 vertices per edge creating subtle wobble, with tops either pointed (bezierVertex peaks) or fractured (random jagged vertices). SECONDARY: Particle debris system — each blade fragments into 80-150 micro-particles (2-6px irregular polygons, 4-7 vertices each, constructed via vertex() with random angular distribution). TERTIARY: Connection threads — when particles are within 40px of each other, draw wobbly lines between them using curveVertex() with 3 control points, creating organic tendrils. Thread opacity inversely proportional to distance. TEXTURE: Background grain layer of 2000 tiny (1-2px) semi-transparent rectangles scattered randomly, refreshed every 180 frames to create subtle analog texture.

## Animation
PHASE 1 (0-15s, frames 0-900): Waveform breathes in place. Each blade oscillates vertically using sin(frameCount * 0.03 + index * 0.2) with amplitude 8-15px (varies per blade). Horizontal compression/expansion: blade widths pulse between 100%-115% using sin(frameCount * 0.015) with cubic easing. Rotation: each blade rotates ±2 degrees using sin(frameCount * 0.025 + index * 0.5). Speed: gentle, 0.5px/frame max vertical drift. PHASE 2 (15-45s, frames 900-2700): Erosion sequence. Blades begin fragmenting from edges inward. Starting at frame 900, outermost blades (left/right extremes) break first — each blade spawns its particle cloud over 60 frames (1 second), particles inherit blade's velocity plus radial burst (2-5px/frame initial velocity, exponential decay to 0.3px/frame over 120 frames). Fragmentation propagates inward at 2 blades per second. Particles fall with gravity (0.08px/frame² acceleration downward) and drift horizontally using Perlin noise (noise(particle.x * 0.01, frameCount * 0.002) mapped to -0.5 to +0.5 px/frame). Connection threads fade in when 30% of blades are fragmented. By frame 2400, all blades are particles. Speed: chaotic, 3-8px/frame during burst, settling to 0.5-2px/frame. PHASE 3 (45s+, frames 2700+): Magnetic reconvergence. Particles experience attraction force toward new center points (5-8 attractor points arranged in warped waveform shape, vertically offset ±60px from original). Attraction force: inversely proportional to distance squared, max 0.4px/frame acceleration. Particles spiral inward using combination of attraction vector + perpendicular tangent (creating vortex motion). As particles cluster near attractors (within 25px), they begin morphing back into blade-like forms through vertex averaging — particles merge into new custom shapes with 10-20 vertices. Reconvergence completes over 20 seconds (frames 2700-3900). New waveform is 70% height of original, more irregular. Speed: accelerating inward, 0.2px/frame initially, peaking at 4px/frame near attractors, then damped sinusoidal settling. LOOP: After reconvergence, piece holds new waveform for 10 seconds (frames 3900-4500), then erosion restarts with different fragmentation pattern (random seed changes).

## Transformation
BLADE TO PARTICLE: Each blade's transformation occurs over 60 frames. Frames 0-20: blade edge vertices begin separating, gaps appear (noise-driven vertex displacement increases from ±3px to ±15px). Frames 20-40: blade splits into 4-6 chunks (vertex clusters), chunks rotate independently (0.5-2 degrees/frame). Frames 40-60: chunks explode into individual particles, opacity drops from 100% to 80% over 15 frames, then holds. PARTICLE CLUSTERING: As particles approach attractors, they gain mass through visual merging — when within 15px of another particle, they form soft connections (metaball-like effect via overlapping semi-transparent shapes). Groups of 8-12 particles coalesce into proto-blade forms using convex hull vertex calculation, refined every 10 frames. OPACITY CURVES: Blades start at 100% opacity. During fragmentation, drop to 85% over 30 frames (linear). Particles hold 75-85% (varies per particle). During reconvergence, merged forms pulse between 80-95% using sin() with 4-second period. SCALE: Particles have inherent scale 100% but visual scale varies 60-140% based on distance from nearest neighbor (isolation = smaller appearance). Reconverged blades are 85-110% of original blade height, with ±15px random variation.

## Rhythm
MACRO: The piece has a 90-second breath cycle (15s stable, 30s dissolution, 20s reconvergence, 10s hold, 15s stable with new form, repeat). Overall arc: tension builds during the unnaturally long erosion phase (viewer anticipates collapse), releases during the chaotic particle scatter, then resolves through slow magnetic pulling. MICRO: Individual blade oscillations are phase-offset by index * 0.2 radians, creating wave-like cascades across the form (apparent wave travels left-to-right at ~8px/frame). Particle emissions are staggered (2 blades/second fragmentation rate = 0.5-second gaps between bursts), creating rhythmic pulses rather than smooth flow. Connection threads flicker in/out as particles cross the 40px proximity threshold, generating visual syncopation. Background grain refreshes every 3 seconds (180 frames at 60fps), providing subtle temporal anchors. Reconvergence spirals have variable angular velocity (faster when distant, slower when near) creating rubber-band tension rhythm.

## Interaction (mouse)
MOUSE X (horizontal position): Controls the horizontal tilt angle of the entire waveform structure, mapped from -15 degrees (left edge) to +15 degrees (right edge) using lerp() with 0.05 smoothing factor for fluid following. This tilt affects both the initial blade arrangement and the attractor positions during reconvergence. MOUSE Y (vertical position): Controls particle gravity strength during erosion phase, mapped from 0.02px/frame² (top of screen — floaty, slow fall) to 0.15px/frame² (bottom — heavy, rapid collapse). Also affects connection thread visibility threshold — at top of screen, threads appear at 60px distance; at bottom, threshold tightens to 25px. MOUSE CLICK: Instantly triggers erosion phase regardless of current timing — if clicked during stable phase, skips ahead to fragmentation. If clicked during reconvergence, reverses the process (particles burst outward again with 3-6px/frame radial velocity). Click spawns a localized shockwave (expanding circle of force, 200px radius, 0.8px/frame expansion, lasting 90 frames) that pushes nearby particles radially outward with force inverse to distance. PASSIVE FALLBACK: When mouse is stationary for >5 seconds, the system gradually returns tilt to 0 degrees (using exponential easing, 0.02 factor) and gravity settles to default 0.08px/frame². Autonomous oscillation takes over mouse Y influence using sin(frameCount * 0.008).
